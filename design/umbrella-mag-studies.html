Soothemade · magazine direction · palette studies

Five palettes, same magazine bones.

The editorial direction in five color treatments. Same layout DNA, different mood. Each has small tweaks beyond the swatches (border weights, type weight, button treatment) so the palette doesn't just sit on top — it shifts the personality.

Locked across all five: type stack (Fraunces, Inter, Caveat), brand voice, the four rooms, the locked tagline. Each study uses colors that already exist in brand/visual-identity.md — warm vintage, bright vintage, the sage/forest theme. Nothing introduced from outside the family.

01.
Terracotta on cream

The default daylight. Calm, considered, balanced.

Cream paper, charcoal ink, terracotta as the singular accent. Sage appears only in supporting roles (sprigs, borders). The most "Soothemade-default" of the five. Reads like a slow literary quarterly.

02.
Forest dark

Cream serif on deep moss paper.

Inverts the contrast. Deep forest green as the paper; cream as the ink. Terracotta accent pops against the green. Reads like an indie literary journal printed at night. Matches the dark theme already used on Notes for continuity.

03.
Harvest

Wheat + mustard ochre. Heritage.

Wheat-colored paper, mustard ochre as the accent, deep umber ink. Sage appears in metadata. Feels like a small folk magazine or an agricultural quarterly. Warmer than the default, more rustic.

04.
Vermillion + moss

Bright vintage. Bolder, more print-feel.

The bright-vintage palette from visual-identity.md. Vermillion as the accent (more saturated than terracotta), forest moss as the structural color. Bolder type weights, a solid-fill CTA, an accent strip on the masthead. More energetic — closer to a design magazine than a literary one.

05.
Quiet monochrome

Almost no color. Restraint as the design.

Cream paper, ink-only typography. Terracotta appears once as a single small dot beside the wordmark. Hairline rules instead of bold borders. Lots of negative space. Centered, narrow content column. Reads like an architectural review or design quarterly — the brand at its quietest.
