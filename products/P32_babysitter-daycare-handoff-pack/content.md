# The Babysitter & Daycare Handoff Pack

*Sixteen pages of handoff documents for daycare, the babysitter, grandparents, and the parents'-night-out. Fill in once. Update by season. Keep one on the fridge, one in the diaper bag.*

---

## A note before you print

Every time you leave your kid with someone, you have a small choice. You can re-explain the whole system, or you can hand them a page. This pack is the page.

Fill it in once. Update it when something changes (a new word, a new fear, a new allergy, a new bedtime). The work is in the first hour. After that, you are mostly handing it over.

*Soothemade*

> *This is a planning tool, not medical advice. For specific medical or care concerns, please follow your pediatric provider's guidance. We do not name medications in this pack. Call the parent first, always.*

---

## How to use this pack

- Print on plain paper. The pack is meant to be marked up.
- Fill it in once, in pen, in the kitchen, with the partner if you have one.
- Make 3 copies. One on the fridge. One in the diaper bag. One in the car.
- Update once per season (4 times a year). Tape it to the calendar.
- For one-off handoffs (a date night, a parents' weekend away), hand the appropriate pages, not all 16.

## The "in case of" page

For the moments that might happen.

```
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
IN CASE OF

A SMALL CUT OR SCRAPE
   1. Calm the kid first.
   2. Rinse with water. Pat dry. Apply a bandage if
      the kid likes bandages, skip if they don't.
   3. Tell the parent at pickup. No need to call.

A FALL
   1. Comfort, then assess. Most falls are nothing.
   2. If the kid is acting like themselves within
      5 minutes, no call needed. Tell parent at pickup.
   3. If the kid hit their head AND is acting
      sleepy, vomiting, confused, or otherwise off,
      call the parent immediately.

A FEVER (per touch, not measured)
   1. Tell the parent. Always.
   2. We do NOT give any medication without the parent
      saying so. Please call us.
   3. Keep the kid in a quiet, cool space.

A RASH
   1. Tell the parent. Always.
   2. We do not apply any cream or lotion without the
      parent saying so.
   3. Watch for: spreading, blistering, fever, or the
      kid scratching open the skin.

VOMITING
   1. Comfort the kid.
   2. Offer small sips of water once the vomiting stops
      for 15 minutes.
   3. Call the parent.

THE KID WILL NOT STOP CRYING (more than 20 minutes)
   1. Try the wind-down ritual. Try a hug. Try outside.
   2. Try the favorite book.
   3. If 20 minutes have passed and nothing is helping,
      call the parent.

THE KID HAS HAD AN ACCIDENT (potty)
   1. Stay calm. Even pause-and-smile.
   2. "It's okay. Bodies are learning. Let's get clean."
   3. Use the spare clothes in the bag.
   4. Put the wet clothes in the wet bag.
   5. No need to call the parent unless asked.

THE KID IS HAVING A TANTRUM
   1. Stay nearby. Don't try to reason with them
      mid-storm.
   2. Try one of the cards from the Tantrum Scripts
      (P16) if you have them.
   3. Wait. Tantrums end. Most last 5-15 minutes.
   4. Repair after, when they are back in their body.

ANY EMERGENCY
   1. 911 first if it's life-threatening.
   2. Call the parent SECOND.
   3. The pediatrician's after-hours line is also on
      the summary page.
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

```
       Time:  _____   Amount: _____   Notes: ___________
   Medication 2 (by purpose, not name): _____________
       Time:  _____   Amount: _____   Notes: ___________

WHAT THE KID CAN TAKE "AS NEEDED" (parent-approved only)

   For ___________: parent will tell you when, and
                    where the bottle is.

RULE: WE DO NOT GIVE ANY MEDICATION WITHOUT THE PARENT
SAYING SO FOR THIS SPECIFIC INSTANCE.

If you think the kid needs something (a fever reducer,
something for an allergic reaction, anything else),
please call the parent first. Always. Even if it
seems urgent. Even if the kid has had this same thing
before. Always.

For genuine emergencies (severe allergic reaction
where the parent isn't reachable in time), follow
the response plan above OR call 911.
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## The daycare-orientation page

For when you are touring a new daycare or interviewing a nanny.

```
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
QUESTIONS TO ASK ON THE TOUR

ABOUT THE FACILITY (for daycare)

   ☐ What is the staff-to-child ratio?
   ☐ How is the facility cleaned and how often?
   ☐ What are the sick-child policies?
   ☐ How are vaccinations handled (theirs and other
     kids')?
   ☐ What's the food situation? Allergies handled how?
   ☐ Can I see the napping area? Outdoor area? Cubbies?
   ☐ What's the daily schedule, roughly?
   ☐ How do you communicate with parents?
   ☐ What's the discipline approach for tantrums,
     biting, hitting?
   ☐ Who is the lead teacher? How long have they
     been there?
   ☐ What's the turnover rate (politely asked)?

ABOUT THE NANNY (for an in-home caregiver)

   ☐ Tell me about your experience with kids age ___.
   ☐ How do you handle a tantrum?
   ☐ What does a typical day look like for you with
     a kid this age?
   ☐ What's your view on screen time?
   ☐ Are you comfortable with our food preferences?
   ☐ How do you handle a sick day (yours and the kid's)?
   ☐ What's your transportation situation?
   ☐ References. Three, minimum. Call all of them.

RED FLAGS TO WATCH FOR

   ✗ Staff who do not look at or engage with kids
     during the tour
   ✗ Crying children no one is attending to
   ✗ Doors propped open into unsecure areas
   ✗ No clear sick-child policy
   ✗ Vague answers to specific questions
   ✗ Pressure to commit immediately
   ✗ Reluctance to share references (nanny)
   ✗ Reluctance to let you observe (daycare)
   ✗ Any feeling, in your body, that something is off

The "feeling something is off" one is real data.
Trust it.
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

## A final note for the parent

A page at the back, just for you.

> *Handing over your kid is hard. Even for an hour. Even to someone you completely trust.*
>
> *You filled in this pack so that the handover does not require you to be in two places at once.*
>
> *The kid will be okay. The caregiver, given this pack, has more information than most parents have on a normal day.*
>
> *Walk to the car. Don't look back. Or look back once, smile, then walk to the car.*
>
> *The day will not be perfect. Tomorrow you will do it again. It gets easier.*
