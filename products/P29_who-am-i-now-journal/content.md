# A 'Who Am I Now' Journal

*Forty-eight pages of prompted journaling for the mother who looks in the mirror and does not immediately recognize herself. Not a self-help book. A book for the version of you who is here, building a new self, on a schedule no one warned you about.*

---

## A note before you start

There is a version of postpartum writing that promises to help you "get yourself back." This journal does not do that.

You are not going back. The version of you before the baby was real. The version of you now is also real. The work is not recovering an old self, it is meeting the new one, who is showing up in pieces, on her own schedule, often when the baby is finally asleep.

This is a journal for that meeting.

It is for any stretch of postpartum or post-postpartum where the language for who you are now has not arrived yet. Year one. Year three. The week your kid started school. The morning you found a pair of pants in the back of the closet that no longer fit a person you no longer are.

*With care, Maya*

> *This is a journaling tool, not therapy or medical advice. If you are in crisis or sustained low mood, please talk to a real provider. The numbers are at the back.*

---

## How to use this journal

- Forty-eight pages, undated. Start whenever.
- Read the prompts when you have ten quiet minutes. Not five. Not three. The journal does not work in three-minute bursts.
- Skip prompts that don't apply. There is no order.
- Some prompts will land hard. Sit on the floor. Take the time.
- Some prompts will feel like nothing today. Try them in three months. The same prompt asked twice, six months apart, will produce different answers.
- Bring it to therapy if you have one. The prompts are good conversation starters.

## The twelve identity prompts

Twelve prompts. One per week if you want a pace. One per month if that is the pace.

There are no right answers. There are no required answers. You can write a paragraph or a sentence. You can write "I don't know" and come back.

### Prompt 1

*When did I last laugh in a way I recognized as my own?*

(Not a polite laugh. Not a laugh-for-the-baby laugh. The laugh that was you, in a way you'd known yourself before. Where? Why?)

### Prompt 2

*What is one thing I want my child to never have to defend about themselves?*

(The thing about you, growing up, that you had to spend years explaining or apologizing for. Write it down. Then write what you are doing, this week, to make sure they don't have to.)

### Prompt 3

*What part of my mother (or main caregiver) do I want to keep?*

(Specific. Not "her kindness." Her exact way of doing one thing. The way she sat on the edge of the bed at night. The way she said hello to the dog.)

### Prompt 4

*What part of my mother (or main caregiver) do I want to leave behind?*

(Same specificity. Not "her anger." The exact thing. The shape of her silence at the dinner table. The phrase she used that I still flinch from.)

### Prompt 5

*If I could give my pre-baby self one piece of information, what would it be?*

(Not "it gets easier." Specific. About something the pre-baby version of me genuinely did not know.)

### Prompt 6

*Who is the friend I have lost track of, who I miss more than I have admitted?*

(Write their name. Write down what stopped the friendship. Write whether you would text them today.)

### Prompt 7

*What is one place I haven't been to in a long time that I want to go again?*

(Could be a literal place. Could be a state of mind. A bar. A library. The kind of conversation. The kind of music.)

### Prompt 8

*If I were 80 years old, looking back at the version of me right now, what would I tell her?*

(Be the 80-year-old. Talk to the current you. Plain language. No clichés.)

### Prompt 9

*What is the smallest thing about my body that I have made peace with?*

(Specific. A scar. A line. A change. Not "my whole body." One small specific thing.)

### Prompt 10

*What is the thing I do for myself that no one knows about?*

(The way you put the coffee down before the first sip. The book on the kitchen counter you read three lines of every morning. The song you sing in the shower. Small things count.)

### Prompt 11

*What is a question I keep asking myself that I have not yet answered?*

(Could be about the job. The marriage. The number of kids. The where-to-live. Write the question, not the answer.)

### Prompt 12

*Who am I now?*

(In one paragraph. Or one sentence. Or one word.)

## The annual ritual

A page to fill in once a year, on a date that matters to you. The same questions. Year over year, the answers shift.

Print or copy. Use it on:

- The baby's birthday (or due date if you'd rather)
- Your birthday
- A specific seasonal anchor (the first day of school, the longest night, your wedding anniversary, your name day)
- A made-up date that matters to no one but you

## The "I don't know" page

A page that is okay to write nothing on.

> *Some questions don't have answers yet. Some pages do not get filled.*
>
> *Sitting with the not-knowing is also the work. Not every page of who-you-are-now resolves into a sentence.*
>
> *If you got to this page and have written nothing in months, that is allowed. The journal will be here. You will come back to it, or you will not, and either is part of what becoming-someone-new looks like.*

## When the journal isn't enough

A short page. Honest.

A journal helps you see your own pattern. It does not, by itself, treat depression, anxiety, intrusive thoughts, or other postpartum mood concerns.

If, as you write in this journal, you notice:

- A sustained low mood that doesn't lift
- Thoughts about hurting yourself
- Thoughts about hurting the baby
- A sustained inability to feel anything for the baby
- A sustained inability to feel anything at all
- Disconnection from reality
- Increasing isolation that feels involuntary

Please talk to a real provider. The journal is for noticing. The treatment is for treating.
