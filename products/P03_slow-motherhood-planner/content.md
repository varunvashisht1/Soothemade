# The Slow Motherhood Weekly Planner

*A 4-week undated planner for the mom who's tired of optimizing. 36 pages, designed to be used badly, on the days you remember.*

---

## How this planner is different (page 1)

Most planners are productivity systems for moms who don't have time for productivity systems.

This isn't that.

There are no time-blocked schedules. No "morning routine" prescriptions. No "5 things to do before 8am." No habit-tracker grid you'll abandon on day three and feel bad about.

What this planner has instead:

- **Four weekly spreads.** One page per week. Not seven.
- **Three questions a day, not twenty.** Most days you'll answer one. That's enough.
- **A monthly reflection** that doesn't ask if you "crushed your goals." It asks where you found two minutes of yourself this month.
- **An intention exercise** at the start that you can do in 15 minutes. Or break into 5-minute pieces. Or skip and come back to. The planner won't notice.

You don't have to use every page. You don't have to use them in order. You don't have to feel bad about the pages you skip.

Slow motherhood doesn't mean doing less. It means refusing to perform what you're not actually doing.

Use it badly. That's how this works.

— *Soothemade*

## Page 2 — A note on "slow"

A friend asked me what "slow motherhood" means. Here's what I said:

It's not a Sunday in a sunbeam with a clean linen apron and a sourdough loaf.

It's a Tuesday at 4pm when the laundry is still in the dryer and the baby is finally asleep and I have to choose between sitting with my coffee for 4 minutes or starting dinner — and I pick the coffee.

That's slow motherhood. The 4 minutes you choose yourself, when there's an equally valid case for the laundry.

Some weeks you'll pick the laundry every time. That's okay too. Slow is not a rule. It's a permission.

This planner is the permission, written down.

```
THE TWO QUESTIONS

What's one thing for me today (even tiny)?
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

What's one thing for the kids today (it can be small)?
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE LIST (do or don't — the planner doesn't care)

☐ ________________________________________________
☐ ________________________________________________
☐ ________________________________________________

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ONE NOTE FROM TODAY (a word, a phrase, a line of dialogue)

___________________________________________________
___________________________________________________

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

HOW I'D RATE TODAY (circle one, no shame)

🙂 mostly okay     😐 fine, that's enough     🌧️ today was hard

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

The intentional design choice: there's space for 3 list items, not 10. Three is the max you should plan in a day with small kids. If you wrote 10, you were lying to yourself. The planner won't help you lie to yourself.

## Page 36 — A closing note

You did this.

I know you might have skipped pages. I know there might be days that look like a tornado went through the page (or no marks at all). I know there's probably a coffee ring on page 19.

That's the planner doing its job.

The point was never the pages. The point was the 60 seconds you took to ask yourself a question. The 30 seconds you spent noticing a small moment. The handful of times you wrote something honest in a quiet corner of a Tuesday.

If those things happened — even once — you've done the thing.

If they didn't happen this month — that's also okay. The planner will still be here next month. So will you.

The undated, no-deadline, no-shame nature of this thing is the entire point. Use it again when you want to. Skip it when you don't. Buy it for a friend who's tired.

Slow is a practice. It takes time. And probably more than one planner.

— *Soothemade*
