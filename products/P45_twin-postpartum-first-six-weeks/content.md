# Twin Postpartum, the First Six Weeks

*Forty pages for the country that singleton planners and twin pregnancy planners do not cover. The first six weeks home with two.*

---

## A note before you start

You came home with two of them.

The first week is mostly survival. The second is when you start to lose the thread of what day it is. The third is when the visitors start to thin out. The fourth is the one where you wonder if you will ever feel rested again. The sixth is the appointment that closes this chapter.

This planner is for that stretch. Not for what twin parenting will look like at six months, when you have your rhythm. For now. For the six weeks where the rhythm is being built, on no sleep, with two of them needing you at the same time.

It is also for the partner. The first six weeks of twin postpartum is a two-person job for nearly everyone. The pages that have a "partner" header are for the second adult to read and write in.

*Soothemade*

> *This is a planning tool, not medical advice. For specific concerns about recovery, feeding, mental health, or your babies' health, please follow your obstetric and pediatric providers' guidance. The planner does not name medications. The mental-health crisis line page is at the back. bookmark it now.*

## What is different for twin postpartum

The list, in plain language. None of these is a "but you can do it" framing. They are the realities.

**Feeding takes twice as long, or differently.** Whether you are exclusively breastfeeding, exclusively bottle-feeding, or mixing, the feeding schedule for two is its own logistical structure. Tandem feeds, alternating feeds, one parent on each baby, none of those work the same as one-baby feeding.

**Sleep is more disrupted, statistically.** Two babies wake on their own schedules. Some twin parents work hard to synchronize them. Some do not. Both are valid. The math is "fewer hours of unbroken sleep" either way.

**Recovery for the parent is sometimes longer.** Multifetal pregnancies often deliver earlier, sometimes with more complications. A twin postpartum recovery is its own thing. Your provider knows the specifics.

**Mental health is more affected.** Postpartum depression and postpartum anxiety occur at higher rates in twin parents than singleton parents. This is well-documented. It is not a moral failing. It is a known pattern. The planner has explicit pages for early-warning signs.

**The village is heavier.** Twin parents need more help than singleton parents. The visitor balance is different. You need more hands and you need them more often.

**Visitor expectations are sometimes weird.** People treat twins as a curiosity. "Oh my god, two." You will hear this many times. The visitor management page has scripts.

**Costs are higher.** Two of many things. The financial weight surfaces faster.

**Comparison is harder.** Two babies the same age, in the same home. They will be different in everything. The comparison instinct is strong. The planner has a permission slip for that.

## Visitor management for twin parents

Twin visitors are different from singleton visitors. People come to see the curiosity. Some of them are helpful. Some of them are not.

```
WHO TO LET IN, AND WHY

   THE HELPER VISITOR (yes):
   - Brings food
   - Does dishes
   - Holds a baby while you eat
   - Stays in the kitchen, not the nursery
   - Leaves when you give the signal

   THE PERFORMANCE VISITOR (limit):
   - Wants photos of the twins
   - Wants to hold both at once
   - Stays for two hours
   - Does not help

WHAT TO SAY TO THE PERFORMANCE VISITOR

   "We are doing 20-minute visits this month. Thank you for understanding."

   "We are not posting photos yet. Thank you for not posting any you took."

   "We need help with [specific task] more than holding right now. Are
   you open to [task]?"

   "We are taking a quiet day. Can we reschedule for [later date]?"

THE WEEKLY VISITOR CAP (worth setting)

   This week's visitor count:                ____________________
   What I will say to the eighth person who asks:
   _______________________________________________

   Whose visits ENERGIZE me:
   _______________________________________________

   Whose visits DRAIN me:
   _______________________________________________
   (Reduce the latter.)
```

## Partner page

For the second adult. The first six weeks of twin postpartum needs both of you, in a way that singleton postpartum sometimes does not.

```
THE WORK SHARE, HONEST

What the recovering parent is doing right now that you may not see:

   - Healing from delivery (often a longer recovery than singleton)
   - Producing milk if breastfeeding (calorically demanding)
   - Carrying the hormonal shift (which is hard, even when nothing is
     "wrong")
   - Sleeping less than the math suggests because they are still alert
     to the babies even during their "off" shift

What you (the partner) are doing that you may not be acknowledging:

   - Picking up nearly everything outside of feeding
   - Managing the visit calendar
   - Doing two diapers for every one feed
   - Carrying your own grief and shock (this is real, even though you
     did not deliver)

THIS IS A SHARED COLLAPSE, NOT A ONE-SIDED ONE

WHAT TO WATCH FOR IN YOUR PARTNER

If any of these is true, the call to the provider's office is yours
to make, on your partner's behalf if needed:

   ☐ They are crying more than once a day, for more than 20 minutes
     at a stretch, past week 2
   ☐ They have not eaten a full meal in 48 hours
   ☐ They are not sleeping when the babies sleep, for more than a few
     nights
   ☐ They have said something like "the babies would be better off
     without me" (this is a 988 / 1-833-852-6262 call)
   ☐ They are not interested in the babies in a way that feels new
   ☐ Their anxiety is consuming hours of every day
   ☐ Your gut is telling you something

WHAT YOU CAN SAY

   - "I will take this one. You sit."
   - "You are doing the hardest thing I have ever seen."
   - "I am calling the provider on your behalf."
   - "We are not going to figure this out tonight. Let me hold both
     of them for an hour while you sleep."

WHAT TO NOT SAY

   - "Just relax."
   - "It will get better." (true, but not a fix-it sentence)
   - "Other people do this with no help." (this is unhelpful and
     usually untrue)
   - "Why are you crying?"

YOUR OWN MENTAL HEALTH

   Partner postpartum depression and anxiety is real. The rates are
   not as high as for the recovering parent, but they are real.

   If you are noticing your own withdrawal, rage, dread, or sense of
   "I cannot do this," that is the moment to talk to your own
   provider too.
```

## A final letter

Six weeks.

You did it. Or you are still in it, reading this on week 4, and you are doing it. The planner ends at the six-week mark because that is a milestone, not because the work is over. The work is just shifting.

By week six, the babies are individuals to you. You can tell their cries apart. You know which one likes to be held a certain way. You know that there is one who screams during the bath and one who loves it. You know things about them that nobody else in the world knows.

The next phase is different. It is sleep training conversations (or not). It is starting solids. It is the dawn of social smiles, the first weight check that they have passed birth-weight by miles, the photos that get easier to take. The planner closes here because the next chapter is its own.

You did the hardest first six weeks of the first year. With two of them. With less sleep than you knew was possible. You showed up. They are here. You are here.

That is the planner. That is the work. That is enough.

*Soothemade*
