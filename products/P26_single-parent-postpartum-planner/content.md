# A Single-Parent Postpartum Planner

*Forty-four pages for the version of you doing this on your own. By choice, by donor, by circumstance, by separation, by loss, by deployment. The planner that does not assume a second adult in the house.*

---

## A note from me

The first time I bought a postpartum planner, I had to mentally translate every page. "Ask your partner to handle the night feeds." "Have your partner take the baby for a walk." "Set up a co-parenting schedule with your partner." On every page, the word "partner" was the assumption I did not have.

About a quarter of mothers in the US are single parents. Many of you are single because the partner is gone. Some of you are single by choice, by donor, by surrogacy. Some of you have a partner who is technically present but functionally not, because of deployment, illness, addiction, or the kind of distance that lives inside a marriage. Some of you became single during pregnancy or after.

This planner is for you. It does not assume the partner. Where help exists in your life, the planner names it specifically (your mother, a paid postpartum doula, the neighbor with a key, the friend who texts every morning) so the village shows up by name, not by the word "partner" that does not apply.

This planner does not have an opinion about how you got here. It just assumes you are here, doing this, and that the work is real and is yours.

*With care, Maya*

> *This is a planning tool, not medical advice. If anything is physically wrong or you are in crisis, please call a real provider or one of the lines at the back.*

## A small framing, before anything else

A single parent in postpartum is doing the work of two roles plus the recovery. There is no scenario in which this is a fair amount of work. The goal of this planner is not to make it fair. It is to make it survivable, and then sustainable, and then something close to good.

Three things I would put at the front of every page:

### One.

Help is not a moral failure. The people who can do this without help are not better. They are usually just better at hiding the cost. Asking for help is the work. Receiving help is also the work.

### Two.

The "village" is real, but it does not arrive on its own. You build it. You build it from named people: a friend, a neighbor, a sibling, a paid helper, a phone hotline. Not "everyone." Specifically these few.

### Three.

You are allowed to spend money on help if you have any. You are allowed to apply for assistance if you don't. The financial-aid page at the back is not a last resort. It is a Tuesday in week three.

## The night-shift solo plan

The hardest, most expensive part of single-parent postpartum is the nights. Two adults can trade. One adult cannot. This page is about making the solo night as efficient as possible.

### The bedside setup

Set up once. Reset every evening before sleep.

```
WITHIN ARM'S REACH OF THE BED, AT NIGHT

☐ Water bottle, full
☐ A snack (nut bar, banana, anything that does not
   require kitchen access)
☐ Phone, charged
☐ Diaper supplies for the bed-side change (or a small
   changing pad)
☐ Two extra burp cloths
☐ A small lamp with a low-light bulb (not the overhead)
☐ A pen and a small piece of paper (for the things you
   need to remember in the morning)
☐ The baby's safe sleep space, within reach
☐ A pacifier or feeding supplies
☐ A small "comfort thing" (a soft pillow, an essential
   oil, anything)
```

### The two-bottle system (if formula or pumped milk)

If you are bottle-feeding, pre-make two bottles each evening for the night feeds. Refrigerate. The night feed becomes:

1. Reach for bottle in the small fridge / cooler bag
2. Brief warming (or room-temp, if pediatrician okays it)
3. Feed
4. Sleep

The 12-minute solo night feed becomes a 5-minute solo night feed.

### The "horizontal whenever possible" rule

If you are nursing, lying down to nurse is fully fine for many parents (look up the Safe Sleep Seven if you co-sleep at all, and read it before relying on it). Horizontal nursing recovers about 40 percent of the sleep that vertical-chair nursing loses.

### The "I will be a person tomorrow" rule

You will be a person tomorrow IF, tonight, you let some things go. The thing you let go tonight is whichever of these is on your list:

```
☐ Doing the dishes
☐ Cleaning up the living room
☐ Replying to texts
☐ Posting an update
☐ Showering before bed (do it in the morning)
☐ Folding the laundry
☐ Anything that is not feeding the baby, feeding yourself,
   and sleeping
```

The household is the next project. The first project is the night.

## The food plan

A page for the boring but important question: what will you eat for six weeks.

### In week 37 of pregnancy (or now, if the baby is here)

```
THE FREEZER, BUILD IT

   ☐ Three meals you actually like, batched and frozen.
     Not aspirational meals. Meals you have eaten and
     liked.
   ☐ Pre-portioned. Single servings. So that "thaw
     dinner" is one move, not five.
   ☐ Snacks. Nuts. Granola bars. Frozen smoothie packs.
   ☐ Easy breakfasts. Steel-cut oats in a jar. Frozen
     pancakes. Whatever you can eat with one hand.

THE PANTRY

   ☐ Three soups in cans (the kind you can eat cold if
     you have to)
   ☐ Crackers
   ☐ Peanut butter or nut butter
   ☐ A box of cereal you do not mind eating dry
   ☐ Three boxes of pasta + sauce
   ☐ Apples and bananas (replace weekly)
```

### The "drop on the porch" script

For whoever you can ask:

```
"Hi! I'm three weeks postpartum and not hosting visits
yet. If you wanted to be amazing, you could drop a meal
on the porch sometime this week. Anything I would eat:
- A roast chicken from the deli
- Soup from anywhere
- A pre-made salad in a box

No need to ring the bell. No need to come in. I'll send
you a thank-you when I surface. Thank you for thinking
of us."
```

Send this to three people. At least one will say yes.

## A small-business / work re-entry page

If you are also self-employed, freelance, or trying to keep some work moving:

### What's realistic

- Week 1: no work. Period.
- Week 2: maybe respond to one critical email per day.
- Week 3: 30 minutes a day if you must.
- Week 4: small projects, no calls.
- Week 5-6: ramp up gently. If you are seeing clients/customers, the first one back is brief.

If you are an employee on leave, this section may not apply. If you can take 12+ weeks of leave, please take it. If you cannot, this section is for you.

### The "out of office" you actually send

```
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Hello,

Thank you for your message. I am on a brief leave for
the birth of my baby and will be slow to respond through
___________ (approximate date).

For urgent matters, please contact ___________.

For non-urgent matters, I will get back to you as soon
as I can. Thank you for your patience.

_______________________

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

Set this BEFORE the baby comes. Set it for two weeks longer than you think you need. You will need the extra week.

### The first three client/customer interactions back

Plan them. Each one is briefer than your usual pre-baby version. Build slowly. Do not start with the hardest client.

## A page for the friends and family

A page to print, hand out, or send. So that "how can I help?" gets a real answer.

```
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

To friends and family,

I'm a single parent in postpartum. Thank you for caring
about us. Here are specific ways to help. I am not asking
for any of these from any specific person. I am giving
you a menu so the asking is easier on both of us.

THE SHORTEST LIST

   ☐ Drop a meal on the porch. No visit required.
     Text me when it's there.

   ☐ Walk the dog ____________ for 30 minutes.

   ☐ Empty my dishwasher. Just that. Just emptying.

   ☐ Sit with the baby for 90 minutes so I can sleep.

   ☐ Drive me to the pediatrician appointment on ______.

   ☐ Pick up groceries from the list I'll send.

   ☐ Bring me one coffee on a Tuesday morning.

   ☐ Just text me at 9am with a single sentence. Don't
     expect a reply. The text itself is the help.

THE SLIGHTLY LARGER LIST

   ☐ Take the baby for a stretch (3+ hours) so I can
     do something else.

   ☐ Come help me sort the paperwork (medical, insurance,
     etc.).

   ☐ Sit with me. Don't do anything. Just be in the
     room. Bring a book.

WHAT WOULD NOT BE HELPFUL RIGHT NOW

   ✗ Opinions about my decisions
   ✗ Visits longer than 90 minutes
   ✗ Visits without food
   ✗ Photos posted of us without my okay
   ✗ "How are you" without time to actually hear it

Thank you. I am doing okay. I will tell you when I'm
ready for more.

   ____________________________

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### If the other parent is here but not functional

This is a harder version of the conversation. A therapist for you (and a family therapist for both of you, if possible) is the gold standard. Until then:

```
WHAT I AM HANDLING (because they cannot right now)

   ____________________________________________

WHAT THEY ARE HANDLING (be honest, even if it's small)

   ____________________________________________

WHAT I WOULD LIKE TO BE TRUE IN 6 MONTHS

   ____________________________________________

WHAT I AM DOING TODAY ABOUT THAT

   ____________________________________________
```

A note: if the other parent is unsafe (substance abuse, harm, neglect), you do not owe them participation in this planner. Please prioritize your safety and the baby's. The Childhelp line is **1-800-422-4453** and is confidential.

## A page that is just for you

A page at the back. To read in a quiet moment.

> *You are doing one of the hardest versions of one of the hardest things humans do.*
>
> *Most of the parenting content in the world assumes two adults. Most of the parenting advice in the world says "ask your partner" as if that is a free move. You are doing it without that.*
>
> *That is not a footnote on your story. That is a central fact about it. You should be more proud of yourself than you currently are. The math suggests you don't fully see it from the inside.*
>
> *Here are three things to write down, if you want, in your own handwriting.*
>
> *One. What is one thing I have done this week that no one will ever know about?*
>
> *Two. What is one thing about myself I am only learning now, in this season?*
>
> *Three. What is one thing the baby will never know about how much I carried this year?*
>
> *Put it down somewhere. The page can hold it.*
