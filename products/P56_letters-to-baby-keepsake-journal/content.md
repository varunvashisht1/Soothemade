# Letters to Baby, a Pregnancy Keepsake Journal

*Forty pages. Thirteen letters across the nine months. For them. For you. For later.*

---

## A note before you start

This journal is the lyrical one.

Most pregnancy planners track. Some plan. This one is for writing. To them. About them. About the year you are alive in while you wait for them. About the people around you who they have not met yet.

You can fill it in slowly. A letter a month, more or less. You can do it all in one Saturday afternoon at the kitchen table. You can leave letters blank that do not call to you. You can write extra in the margins. You can paste in things that are not asked for.

There is no right way to fill in this journal. There is only the way you do it.

When the baby is here, you can keep this on a shelf. You can give it to them when they are old enough to ask. You can read it on the morning of their wedding. You can pull it out the first time they break your heart, just to remember the version of yourself that wrote to them before they could refuse a hug.

Whatever you write becomes a thing they have. Or a thing you have. Either is the right answer.

*Soothemade*

> *This is a keepsake journal, not a medical record. For specific concerns about the pregnancy, please follow your obstetric provider's guidance.*

---

## How to use this journal

- Print on good paper if you can. Card stock for the pocket pages.
- Write in pen, the way you would write a letter you intended to be read.
- The letters are loosely chronological but you do not have to do them in order.
- Some letters take five minutes. Some take an hour. Both are fine.
- The pocket pages can hold the actual ultrasound print and the actual hospital bracelet. Cut a small slit and tuck them in. Or scan them and print them in.

A small principle: do not edit yourself for the future reader. Write the honest thing. The kid does not need a polished version of you. They need the real one.

## A final note

You wrote to a person you had not met yet.

For most of human history, the version of yourself who was pregnant with the version of yourself who is now your child has been lost. You did not let that happen to you. You wrote it down.

Whatever the kid grows up to do, whether they read this journal at twelve or at thirty or never, whether they laugh at it or cry over it, the act of having written it is itself part of the story. They will know, on some level, that you wrote to them before they could refuse the gesture.

The journal closes here. The conversation continues for the rest of your life together.

We are so glad they are here.

*Soothemade*
