# A Daily Notes Journal

*Sixty pages. Six months of tracking. Looks like any other notebook to anyone who is not you.*

---

## A note before you start

You are doing something private.

You picked this journal because you wanted a place to track the body without a cover that announces it to the kitchen. The in-laws can flip through this. The roommate can pick it up. The coworker can see it on your desk. None of them will know what it is unless you tell them.

You will know. You knew when you bought it. That is the whole design.

This journal is structured around six months of cycle-tracking, with the data fields you actually need (morning temperature, body notes, mood, energy, symptoms), but labeled in plain words that do not say "ovulation" or "cycle day" or "fertility." The decode page at the back tells you which neutral field maps to which clinical data point. You can keep the decode page. You can tear it out.

There is no judgment in this journal. There is no projected timeline. There is no "this is how long it should take." There is just the place to write things down, in your own quiet, for as long as you need it.

*With care, Maya*

> *This is a tracking journal, not medical advice. For specific concerns about your fertility, please consult a reproductive endocrinologist or your general provider. This journal does not interpret your data or predict ovulation. It is a place for the data to live.*

---

## How to use this journal

- Fill in the journal in the morning, before you get out of bed, before the day's noise.
- Each "month" has six pages: an overview, four weekly spreads, a reflection, and a planning page for the next month.
- The neutral language is intentional. Use the journal at the breakfast table without explanation.
- A short decode page is at the back. It tells you how the fields map to fertility data. Keep it tucked in, tear it out, or memorize it and toss it.
- After six months of tracking, the journal has a "what to do with this data" page that includes a routing line to a provider.

A small note: the buyer is the person who knows what each field means. The casual reader does not. That is the privacy. Respect it for yourself. You do not owe anyone an explanation of why you keep a journal.

## What each field is

This is the decode page. It is also at the back, if you want to keep one copy and rip out the other.

```
NEUTRAL FIELD NAME              WHAT IT IS, CLINICALLY
─────────────────────────       ─────────────────────────
"Morning temperature"           Basal body temperature (BBT), taken
                                first thing on waking, before any
                                movement, with a thermometer.

"Today's body note"             Cervical fluid / cervical mucus
                                observation. Texture and amount.

"Mood"                          Mood tracking (useful for hormonal
                                pattern recognition).

"Energy"                        Energy level, 1 to 5.

"Sleep"                         Quality and hours of sleep.

"Movement"                      Exercise or physical activity today.

"Drink"                         Alcohol and caffeine (useful baseline).

"Date marker"                   Day in the cycle, counted from
                                day 1 (start of period).

"Notes"                         Anything else, ovulation pain, headache,
                                breast tenderness, intimate timing.

"Test"                          Result of any ovulation predictor or
                                pregnancy test, recorded just by line.
```

If you tear out one copy, keep the other. The fields will get muscle-memory after a month.

## Patterns to look for

This is a reference page. It is not interpretation. The interpretation is the provider's job. This is what some people notice in their own data.

**The temperature shift.** A sustained rise of a few tenths of a degree in the "morning temperature" field that lasts more than a few days often signals something has changed in the cycle. Some people notice this around day 14 to 21 of a cycle. Others notice it earlier or later, or not at all.

**The body-note shift.** The texture of the "body note" field often changes throughout the cycle. Many people notice that it gets stretchier and clearer around mid-cycle, then changes again later.

**Cycle length.** "Day 1 to day 1" gives you cycle length. Most people have cycles between 21 and 35 days. Some have longer or shorter cycles. Wide variation between cycles can be useful information for a provider.

**The luteal-phase pattern.** After the temperature shift, the "morning temperature" usually stays elevated until the next period starts. The length of this elevated stretch (often 10 to 14 days for many people) is sometimes useful clinical information.

**Drink patterns.** Sleep, alcohol, and caffeine all affect body data. Tracking them helps separate "the data was different because of the cycle" from "the data was different because I was hungover."

## When to talk to a provider

The journal is a tracking tool. A provider is the right next step in several situations.

**Talk to a provider if:**

- ☐ You have been tracking and trying for 12 months if you are under 35.
- ☐ You have been tracking and trying for 6 months if you are 35 or older.
- ☐ You have very irregular cycles (much shorter than 21 days or much longer than 35).
- ☐ You have a history of conditions that affect fertility (your provider will know).
- ☐ You have a history of pregnancy loss.
- ☐ Your provider has previously suggested fertility evaluation.
- ☐ You want to be evaluated regardless of the timeline.

**What to bring:**

- The journal (the data is the point of the journal).
- A list of your specific questions.
- Any prior reports if you have them.

**Who to see:**

- Start with your gynecologist or general provider.
- They may refer you to a reproductive endocrinologist (RE).
- An RE specializes in fertility evaluation and treatment.

This journal is the prep work, not the treatment.
