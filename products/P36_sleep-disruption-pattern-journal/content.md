# A Sleep-Disruption Pattern Journal

*Two weeks of logging. Not a method. A way to see what's actually happening before you decide what to do about it.*

---

## A note before you start

Your kid's sleep has changed. Maybe slowly. Maybe overnight. Maybe in a way that feels like the previous version is gone and is not coming back.

You are tired. You have read forty articles. You have considered sleep training. You have considered not sleep training. You have considered asking the pediatrician. You have considered just waiting it out. Your partner has an opinion. Your mother has an opinion. The internet has fifty methods, each one for sale.

This journal is the step before any of that.

For two weeks, you log what is actually happening. Bedtime. Wakings. Naps. Mood. What you tried. What worked, if anything. By the end of two weeks, you have something the methods cannot give you: data about your specific kid in your specific house.

What you do with the pattern, after, is yours to decide. Maybe with your partner. Maybe with your pediatrician. Maybe with the consultant you are about to hire. Maybe you just wait it out, because now you know waiting is what you are choosing.

*With care, Maya*

> *This is a logging tool, not medical advice. For specific concerns about your child's sleep, breathing, or behavior, please call your pediatric provider. This journal does not endorse or describe any sleep-training method.*

---

## How to use this journal

- Print on plain paper. Keep the current week's pages on the kitchen counter or the bedside table.
- Fill in the daily page in the morning, while the night is still fresh. Or fill it in just before bed. Whichever you will actually do.
- The bullet points are starting places, not requirements. If your kid does not nap, skip the nap line. If you do not have a partner, skip the partner line.
- Do the weekly review on Sundays, or whichever day works for your house. The review is the most useful page in the journal.

---

## This journal does not tell you what to do

It is worth being explicit.

This journal does not recommend a sleep-training method. It does not tell you whether to use one or not. It does not name a method. It does not have an opinion about cry-it-out, no-cry, gradual extinction, chair method, pickup-putdown, or any other named approach.

The journal also does not predict the future. It does not say "by week six this regression will pass." It does not say "if you do this, your kid will sleep through." It does not even say "this is a regression." Those are determinations you and your provider can make from the data.

What the journal does:

- Records what happened, on the day it happened.
- Reflects the data back to you each week.
- Names some things that sometimes shift at certain ages.
- Names the situations where the right next step is the pediatrician, not the next method.

What you do with the pattern is your call.

## What might be happening at this developmental stage

This is reference information, not a prescription. The ages are approximate. Every kid is different. The journal lists them so you can recognize them in the data. It does not tell you what to do about any of them.

**Around 3 to 4 months.** Sleep cycles often mature, which means light sleep stretches feel different. Sometimes called the "4-month regression," though not every baby experiences it.

**Around 6 months.** Solids often start around now. Eating patterns shift. Some sleep shifts come with that.

**Around 8 to 10 months.** Separation awareness develops. Many babies start crying when a parent leaves the room, including at bedtime. Common to see a sleep shift.

**Around 12 months.** Some babies drop from two naps to one. The transition can take weeks of inconsistency.

**Around 18 months.** A common burst of language and motor skill. Some sleep shifts.

**Around 2 years.** Some toddlers move from one nap to no nap. The transition is rarely clean.

**Around 3 years.** Imagination develops, and with it nightmares and night fears sometimes start.

**Teething.** Can affect sleep for a few nights here and there, at no predictable age.

**Illness.** Affects sleep. Usually returns to baseline after the illness passes, give or take a week.

**Travel + time zones.** Disrupts sleep. Usually returns to baseline after a week or so of being home.

**Big life events.** New sibling, move, daycare start, parent traveling. Sleep often shifts.

## When to call the pediatrician

The journal is not a medical tool. But it is the right move to pull the journal out and call the pediatrician if any of these are true.

```
CALL THE PEDIATRICIAN IF:

☐ My baby's breathing during sleep sounds different.
   Pauses, gasping, very loud snoring (especially in toddlers),
   chest sucking in.

☐ My kid is snoring loudly every night, even when not sick.

☐ My kid sleeps with their mouth open every night.

☐ My kid is having sleep terrors that come from a deep sleep, often
   in the first half of the night, and they cannot be woken from them.

☐ My kid is wetting the bed at an age and pattern that concerns me.

☐ My kid wakes screaming in pain that does not have an obvious cause.

☐ My kid has reflux that is significantly affecting sleep.

☐ Sleep disruption appeared with new symptoms (fever, rash, vomiting,
   refusal to eat or drink, lethargy).

☐ My kid is meaningfully struggling during the day, can't be awake,
   can't focus, is having mood or behavioral changes.

☐ My kid's growth has slowed (you would see this on the pediatric
   growth chart).

☐ I am no longer functioning, and the lack of functioning is starting
   to scare me.

WHEN I CALL, I WILL BRING:
   - This journal (the data is the point)
   - My specific question, written down
   - My partner's questions if any
   - A list of what I have tried
```

## A final note

You logged for two weeks. That is harder than it sounds when you are this tired. Most people give up after day three.

What you have now is a record of what is actually happening, not what you remember through the haze. You can read the pattern. You can show it to your partner. You can take it to the pediatrician. You can take it to a sleep consultant if you decide that is the right next step. You can also decide to do nothing and just wait, with more confidence that "waiting" is what you are actually choosing.

The journal runs out. The sleep, eventually, gets better. Not always on the timeline you wanted. But it does.

If you are still here in two weeks, you can re-print and run the journal again. The data will be different. That is also useful.

You are doing the work. The kid is going to be okay. So are you.

*With care, Maya*
