# A Newborn Sleep Log

*Sixty pages of sleep-and-feed tracking for the first year. One-handed, in the dark, with no shame and no math. Built for the version of you at 3am who can barely see.*

---

## A note before you start

Around week three of my second baby, someone in my family asked me how he was sleeping. I had no answer. Not a "he sleeps four hours then wakes" kind of no-answer. A "I genuinely have no idea what happened last night" kind of no-answer. I had been awake too many times in a row to keep a coherent count.

That was the night I started writing things down on the back of an envelope on the nightstand. Just times. Just to have something. By the end of the week I had a real picture for the first time, and could finally answer the question.

This log is the version of that envelope, built for the dark.

It is not a sleep training program. It does not tell you what to do. It does not score your baby's sleep against any chart. It only asks you to write down what actually happened, one line at a time, so you stop trying to guess.

*Soothemade*

> *This log is a tracking tool, not medical advice. If your baby is not gaining weight, is sleeping unusually much, is hard to wake for feeds, or you are worried, call your pediatrician. The trusted numbers are at the back.*

## How to use this log

- Use one daily page at a time. Sixty pages total, undated. Use them as a stretch of two months, or spread them across the year, or skip weeks.
- Fill in as you go, not later. Times are easier to capture in the moment than to reconstruct.
- One-handed handwriting is fine. Sideways notes are fine. Cross-outs are fine.
- The page is not a scorecard. The baby is not a project. You are looking at patterns, not performance.

### A note on safety

The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends:

- Babies sleep on their back, on a firm flat surface, until age one.
- No loose bedding, pillows, bumpers, or soft toys in the sleep space.
- Room-sharing (not bed-sharing) for at least the first six months.
- A pacifier at naps and bedtime (after breastfeeding is established) may reduce SIDS risk.

If you have decided to bed-share for any reason, look up the Safe Sleep Seven guidelines and read them before you do. We are not the place to ask whether it is safe in your specific situation. Your pediatrician is.

## The regression pages

Sleep regressions show up almost universally at certain ages. Knowing they are coming does not make them easier, but it does mean you do not have to think "what did I break?" each time. You did not break anything. The baby's brain is doing a developmental sprint.

There is a one-page reference for each regression in the printed log.

### The four-month regression

What happens: around 3-5 months, the baby's sleep cycles mature. Light sleep becomes lighter. They start waking between cycles instead of bridging them.

What's normal:

- Many more night wakings, often hourly
- Naps that suddenly seem to last only 30 minutes
- A baby who fell asleep independently a month ago now needing help again
- Two to six weeks of this

What helps:

- A consistent wind-down (bath, lights low, the same song)
- Drowsy-but-awake placement, if you have the energy
- A dim, quiet, cool room
- Patience (not the inspirational kind, the resigned kind)

What does not help:

- Forcing a sleep training method during the regression itself
- Researching "is something wrong with my baby" at 3am
- Comparing notes with the friend whose baby slept through this

### The six-month regression

Often coincides with starting solids, sitting up, and teething. Many of the same symptoms. Many of the same solutions.

### The eight-to-ten-month regression

Coincides with separation anxiety arriving for real. The baby is now object-permanence aware and knows you exist even when you leave the room. They protest. Loudly.

What helps:

- A short, predictable goodbye ritual
- Brief absences during the day, not just at bedtime
- A lovey or comfort object (after 12 months, per AAP)
- A repeat of the consistent wind-down

### The twelve-month regression

Often coincides with first steps and language explosion. Often coincides with dropping to one nap (which usually doesn't fully settle until around 18 months).

What helps:

- Resisting the urge to drop the nap too early. Most 12-month-olds still need two naps.
- A consistent bedtime, even if the morning nap is moving around.

## The nap reset

A two-page reference for when something has shifted and the daytime is in disarray. Print it. Tape it to the fridge if you have to.

### When to reset

- You don't recognize what the days look like anymore
- The baby is overtired by 4pm every day
- Naps are happening but bedtime has become a nightmare
- A vacation, illness, or travel has scrambled the schedule

### How to reset (5 days, gently)

**Day 1**, observe. Just write down what is happening. Do not try to change anything. You're gathering data.

**Day 2**, fix only the wake-up time. Same time, give or take 15 minutes. Light the room. Out of the crib.

**Day 3**, hold the wake-up time AND offer the first nap at the same time each day. Even if the baby fights it. Even if it's a 20-minute nap.

**Day 4**, hold those two anchors, and now anchor bedtime. Pick a time and start the wind-down the same way each night.

**Day 5**, observe again. The pattern is usually starting to reform.

You are not training. You are scaffolding. The baby finds the rhythm in the structure you offer.

## Is this normal? A monthly reference

For each age, what a pediatrician would describe as a typical range. These are wide ranges, not targets. Your baby is in the range or near it. You don't need to optimize.

### 0-1 month

- Total sleep: 14-17 hours per 24-hour day
- Longest stretch: 2-4 hours, almost always interrupted by feeding
- Day vs night: undifferentiated
- Naps: many, irregular, short

### 1-3 months

- Total sleep: 13-16 hours
- Longest stretch: 4-6 hours starts appearing, especially toward 3 months
- Day vs night: starts to emerge around 6-8 weeks
- Naps: 4-5 per day, shorter to longer

### 3-6 months

- Total sleep: 12-15 hours
- Longest stretch: 6-8 hours by 5-6 months for many (NOT all)
- Day vs night: established
- Naps: 3-4 per day, more consistent length

### 6-9 months

- Total sleep: 12-14 hours
- Longest stretch: 8-10 hours possible
- Naps: 2-3 per day, settling into morning and afternoon

### 9-12 months

- Total sleep: 11-14 hours
- Naps: 2 per day, often around 9-10am and 1-2pm
- Night feeds: often phased out (medically, not necessarily by your baby)

### Where to ask

Your pediatrician. Not the internet. Not a sleep consultant on social media. Bring this log to a real provider if you have questions.
