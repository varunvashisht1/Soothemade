# Adoption Wait + Welcome Pack

*Thirty pages for the pregnancy that does not show.*

---

## A note before you start

You are becoming a parent. The route is one most planners do not cover.

Maybe you are in the early months of the home study. Maybe you have been waiting for a match for a long time, and the wait is starting to feel longer than what people understand. Maybe you got a call that this could be the one, and now you are reading this in a hurry. Maybe a child is arriving in your house this week with a duffel bag and a social worker.

This planner is for that route. It is not a manual on how to adopt. The agency, the lawyer, the social worker do that. This is the planning surface.

The brand voice in this planner respects the complexity. There is grief in many adoption journeys, sometimes from infertility, sometimes from prior loss, sometimes from the birth family's loss that is the necessary other half of every adoption. There is also joy. The planner holds both.

*Soothemade*

> *This is a planning tool, not legal or social-work advice. Your agency, attorney, and social worker are the right resources for the procedural and clinical aspects of your adoption. The crisis-line page is at the back. Infertility, prior loss, and birth-family grief are real parts of many adoption journeys. perinatal mental-health care applies to adoptive parents too.*

---

## How to use this planner

- The planner is not chronological. You can use it in any order.
- The "how we will talk about our family" page is worth reading early. The language you use now will set the tone for the long term.
- The grief page is optional. If your path did not include infertility or prior loss, skip it. If it did, the page is here.
- The match-call page is brief on purpose. The call, if and when it happens, is mostly fast.

## This is its own pregnancy

A page to come back to.

```
A REMINDER

You are pregnant in a different sense.

You are growing the parts of yourself that will hold a child. You are
making decisions about how you will parent, about what your family
will look like, about how money flows, about how the room will be set
up. You are reading books. You are practicing the things you will do.
You are scared. You are excited. You are tired of being asked when
the baby is coming.

Friends will assume you are not "really" pregnant because there is no
visible bump. Some will forget you are in this process at all. Others
will treat the wait as if it is shorter than it is.

This planner is one of the things naming the truth: you are
preparing for a child. That preparation is its own season. It deserves

_______________________________________________

A REMINDER: A FAILED MATCH IS A REAL LOSS. NOT A "MAYBE" LOSS. A
REAL ONE. THE PEOPLE WHO COMPARE IT TO "JUST A DELAY" OR "JUST A
DISAPPOINTMENT" ARE NOT PEOPLE WHO HAVE BEEN HERE.

THE GRIEF IS ALLOWED. WE WILL NOT TELL YOU IT HAPPENED FOR A REASON.
WE WILL NOT TELL YOU THE NEXT MATCH IS THE RIGHT ONE.

WE WILL TELL YOU THIS LOSS DESERVES THE TIME IT NEEDS.
```

A note on respect for the birth family: even when a match falls through because the birth parent chose to parent, that is the birth family's right and a legitimate choice. The adoption community has language for this: "we wish them well" rather than "they backed out." Holding that respect, even in grief, is part of the work.

## How we will talk about our family

This page sets the language now, before the child can understand it, so the language is already natural by the time they are old enough.

```
LANGUAGE WE WANT TO USE

   ☐ "Our family is a family that began through adoption."
   ☐ "She is our daughter. She has a birth mother, who chose this
     family for her."
   ☐ "He has two mothers, his birth mother and me."
   ☐ "Our family came together when ..."
   ☐ Other:
       _______________________________________________

LANGUAGE WE WANT TO AVOID (and why)

   "REAL MOM / REAL DAD"
       Why: implies the adoptive parent is not real. Both are real,
       in different ways. Use "birth mother" + "mom" / "adoptive
       parent" + "mom" depending on context.

   "GAVE UP" or "GAVE AWAY"
       Why: implies indifference. Adoption advocates and adult
       adoptees often prefer "made an adoption plan" or "placed
       for adoption" or simply "the choice they made."

   "LUCKY" (about the child)
       Why: implies the child should be grateful. Adoption is a
       loss for the child, even when it is also a gift. Use
       "loved" or "wanted" rather than "lucky."

   "OUR OWN" (to distinguish biological from adopted children, if
   you have both)
       Why: implies the adopted child is not "our own." If you have
       both, you have "two children." Full stop.

   "CHOSEN" as the only frame
       Why: while "we chose you" can be loving, it can also imply
       the child was selected like a product. Use sparingly and in
       context.

WHAT WE WILL TELL OUR CHILD, IN AGE-APPROPRIATE WAYS

   At toddler age:                 simple, "our family came together
                                   through adoption, you grew in
                                   another mom's belly, and we
                                   became your family"

   At school age:                   more detail, including the birth
                                   family's situation as the child can
                                   handle it

   At teenage / adult:              full information, including
                                   anything we have shielded them
                                   from earlier

WHAT WE WILL NOT SAY

   ☐ We will not present the adoption as a fairy tale
   ☐ We will not present the birth family negatively
   ☐ We will not require gratitude
   ☐ We will not tell strangers their full story (they get to decide
     who to tell, as they grow)

A NOTE: ADULT ADOPTEES HAVE WRITTEN EXTENSIVELY ABOUT THE LANGUAGE
THEY WISH HAD BEEN USED IN THEIR CHILDHOODS. THEIR VOICES MATTER
MORE THAN OUR INSTINCTS. SEEK OUT ADULT-ADOPTEE-LED RESOURCES.
```

## A final letter

You waited. You filed the paperwork. You wrote the essays. You answered the questions. You went through the inspections. You wired the funds. You took the calls. You held the disappointment of the times that did not become. You held the hope of the times that might.

A child is on the way to you, or has just arrived, or will arrive, on a timeline that you did not write.

Whatever the next year of your family looks like, you got here through a different door than most. That door is just as real. The family that walks through it is just as real.

The first year is hard for most adoptive families. The bonding is sometimes immediate and sometimes slow. The post-adoption depression is real (and yes, adoptive parents experience postpartum-style mood disorders, the research is clear on this. if you are not okay, the perinatal mental-health line applies to you too). The questions from strangers are constant. The birth-family relationship is its own ongoing thing.

You are doing it. The planner closes here. The family begins.

*Soothemade*
