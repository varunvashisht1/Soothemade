# A Returning-to-Work Plan

*Thirty-six pages for the broader return-to-work transition. The conversation, the first day, the morning routine, the identity shift, the ninety-day check-in. Built for the version of you who is not the same worker you were a year ago.*

---

## A note before you start

The return to work after a baby is one of the least-supported transitions in modern life. The workplace assumes you are roughly the same person who left. You are not. The body has done a thing. The hormones are doing a thing. The identity is shifting. The schedule has been rebuilt from scratch.

This planner is the structured version of the conversation no one had with you about what to expect.

The Back-to-Work Pumping Planner (P17) handles feeding logistics. This is the broader question: the schedule, the identity, the meetings, the new dynamic with whoever you used to be at work.

*With care, Maya*

> *This is a planning tool, not employment law advice. For specific questions about leave, accommodation, or discrimination, please talk to HR, an employment lawyer, or your state's department of labor.*

## The manager conversation

Three scripts, depending on the situation.

### Script 1, the supportive manager

```
"Thanks for setting this time. A few things I wanted to
flag for the return:

  - I'll be back on _________ at _________ AM.
  - My calendar is open by _________.
  - I'd like to ease into meetings the first week.
    one or two a day, max.
  - [If pumping: I'll need three short pump breaks during
    the day. I'll put them on my calendar.]
  - I'd appreciate a brief catch-up with you and the team
    so I can get up to speed without making everyone
    repeat themselves.
  - I'm planning to start on full days but will reassess
    after the first two weeks.

Is there anything you'd like me to know before I'm back?
Any changes I should be aware of?"
```

### Script 2, the manager who has not been through this

```
"I appreciate you wanting to support the return. A
few things that will help us both:

  - The first two weeks back are typically the hardest.
    I may be a little slower than usual. I'd appreciate
    your patience as I get back up to speed.
  - I'd like to be on calendar for the most important
    meetings, but I'm asking to skip recurring meetings
    that don't require my presence for the first two
    weeks.
  - [If pumping: I'll need three short breaks a day for
    pumping. I'll add them as recurring blocks. They are
    legally protected, but more importantly, they're
    short and not negotiable.]
  - I'll do my best work on focused tasks, less great
    on rapid context-switching the first weeks.

What I'd appreciate from you:

  - Direct feedback if something is not working. I'd
    rather know early.
  - Patience on response times my first 2 weeks.
  - Clarity on what's high priority and what can wait.

Does that work? Anything you'd like to add?"
```

### Script 3, the manager who has been less than supportive

```
"I want to be clear about a few things ahead of the
return.

I'm returning on _________. The protections I have
around lactation breaks, doctor's appointments related
to postpartum, and reasonable accommodation are in
place under [federal law, state law, or company policy
, you fill in what applies].

I'd prefer to handle this collaboratively. What I'm
asking for is:

  - A calendar that allows for pump breaks at roughly
    _____, _____, _____.
  - The first two weeks at a slightly reduced meeting
    load.
  - Communication through email or chat when possible,
    so I have written records of expectations.

If there's a concern about my work, I'd like to discuss
it directly. If there isn't, I'd appreciate the support
to make this transition successful."
```

(This one is for the situation where you may need a paper trail. It is firm but not aggressive. If the response is hostile, your next conversation is with HR, and you should keep notes.)

## The first-day-back plan

What the first day actually looks like.

### The morning of

```
THE NIGHT BEFORE
   ☐ Pack the bag tonight (not at 6am tomorrow)
   ☐ Lay out clothes
   ☐ Confirm the daycare drop-off plan
   ☐ Set two alarms
   ☐ Talk to the partner about the morning split
   ☐ Eat dinner sitting down

THE MORNING OF
   ☐ Wake at the same time you would on a normal work day
   ☐ Coffee while feeding the baby OR coffee before
   ☐ Eat breakfast. The first day at the desk is not the
     day to skip breakfast.
   ☐ Allow yourself an extra 30 minutes for the unexpected
     thing (a blow-out diaper, the keys lost, the baby
     crying at the door)
```

### The drop-off

```
THE DROP-OFF SCRIPT

To the caregiver:
   "I've packed everything in the bag. The handoff sheet
   is in the front pocket. If anything comes up, my work
   number is on the sheet. I'll be there at pickup at
   _____."

To the baby:
   "Mama is going to work. I'll see you at ___ o'clock.
   I love you. Have a fun day."

To yourself:
   "I am going to walk to the car without looking back.
   If I look back, I will not be able to leave."
```

### The drive in

If you cry, you cry. That is normal. It happens to a lot of mothers on day one. It doesn't mean anything is wrong.

What helps:

- A specific podcast or playlist (not something that will make you cry harder)
- Calling a friend who has done this before
- Not calling the caregiver every ten minutes (set a "I won't check until lunch" rule, ish)

### The first hour at the desk

```
☐ Don't open all the emails at once. Open three at a
   time. Triage.
☐ Find the bathroom. Find the pump room (if applicable).
   Find the coffee.
☐ Say hello to whoever you'd normally say hello to.
☐ Let them ask the baby questions briefly, then redirect.
   ("She's great. How was your year? What's been on
   fire?")
☐ The work will still be there. Don't try to "catch up"
   in the first hour. You will not.
```

### The first day, end-of-day check-in

Before you leave:

```
☐ One task I actually finished today: _________________
☐ One thing I want to follow up on tomorrow: __________
☐ One thing I learned: ________________________________
☐ How am I, on a scale of 1-10 (no judgment): ____
☐ What do I need to bring with me tomorrow that I
  didn't have today: _________________________________
```

## The "I am also a different worker now" page

A page to read once.

You are not the same worker you were a year ago. This is not a deficit. It is information.

### What may have shifted

- **Your bandwidth.** Less context-switching tolerance. More focused-task capacity.
- **Your values.** A meeting that doesn't matter feels different now. Sentences like "let's circle back" register as cost, not norm.
- **Your patience.** Both directions. Less patience for office politics. More patience for the actual hard problems.
- **Your priorities.** The 7pm "urgent" email is now genuinely not as urgent. The pediatrician's appointment is.
- **Your speed.** May be slower in raw output. Often faster at sensing what actually matters.

### What that means, practically

- You may be a better worker in some ways. (Sharper. Less interested in performance work.)
- You may be a worse worker in others. (Less available at 8pm. Less interested in three-hour meetings.)
- The combination is real. The combination is what you bring.

### What to do about it

- Don't apologize for the changes. The work was already a system. Now it has new constraints. That's the data.
- Renegotiate scope where you can. "This used to be a 3-month project. With my current capacity, it's a 4-month project. Or, with extra help, it's still 3."
- Identify the work that is uniquely you. Lean into that. Hand off the work that is not.
- Notice which colleagues now drain you and which energize you. The list may have shifted.

## A "this isn't working" page

A page that doesn't assume you'll stay. Honest options.

If, after 90 days, you've decided the current setup isn't working, your options include:

### Negotiation

Most arrangements can be renegotiated. Hours. Days. Remote vs in-office. Scope. Team. Sometimes the company will surprise you. Often they would rather adjust than lose you.

```
THE NEGOTIATION ASK, IN ONE PARAGRAPH

"I've spent the first 90 days back evaluating what's
working and what isn't. The thing that isn't working
is _________________. The change I'd like to propose
is _________________. Here is how I think it would
work, and here is the impact on the business: _________
________________________________________________________

I'm happy to discuss alternatives. I'm asking for this
to make my work here sustainable, which is in both our
interests."
```

### Reducing scope

You may not need to leave the job. You may need to reduce the job.

- Drop a project, even if no one has officially said you can.
- Decline a meeting series, with a polite explanation.
- Push back on the "urgent" that isn't actually urgent.

### Switching internally

A different role, in the same company. Often a much easier transition than leaving. Often the path many returning parents take.

### Leaving

It is allowed. It is not a failure. It is a reasonable response to a job that does not fit the parent you have become.

If you decide to leave:

- Give appropriate notice. Two weeks if you have a normal relationship. Longer if you have a leadership role. Shorter if the workplace has been hostile.
- Take the PTO you've accrued before you leave (in many states this is paid out anyway).
- Continue health insurance via COBRA if needed.
- Don't burn bridges. The world is small. The person you yelled at in your exit interview will show up again in five years.

### Going freelance / consulting

For some, this becomes the version of "work" that actually fits. Flexible hours. Project-based. Higher hourly rate. Less benefits.

This deserves its own conversation, often with an accountant. Worth exploring before deciding.

### Staying home for a stretch

Also a real option. Not a failure. Not a regression. A legitimate choice that many parents make for some period of time. Often with a return-to-paid-work plan eventually.

If this is the choice:

- Talk to a financial advisor before, not after.
- Plan for the gap on a resume. There are ways to talk about it.
- Stay in touch with your network. The hardest part of returning later is the network, not the skills.

## A small final note

A page to read on a hard day.

> *Returning to work after a baby is one of the harder things our culture asks of us, and asks it largely without scaffolding. Most of the people in your office have not done what you are doing.*
>
> *You are not behind. You are not weaker. You are doing the work of two roles, in parallel, with one body, on broken sleep.*
>
> *Three months from now, the routine will mostly settle. Six months from now, you will not believe how hard the first three were.*
>
> *In the meantime: take the small care. Eat at lunch, sitting down. Go to the bathroom when you need to. Drink water. Step outside once a day, even briefly.*
>
> *The work will still be there.*
