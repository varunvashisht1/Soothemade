# Pediatrician Visit Prep Cards

*Twelve cards, one per well-baby visit through age two. Walk in with the questions. Walk out with the answers written down.*

---

## A note from the maker

The well-baby visits arrive faster than you expect. By the time you have made the appointment, gotten the car seat in, and figured out which onesie they will not throw up on this morning, you have forgotten the four questions you wanted to ask.

These cards are the prep work. One card per visit. You fill in the "what to bring" section the night before. You fill in the "what they said" section right after, before you drive home.

After two years, you have a record. Of what was measured. Of what they recommended. Of what you asked and what you got back. It is yours.

*Soothemade*

> *This is a planning tool, not medical advice. For specific concerns about your child's health, please follow your pediatric provider's guidance. The cards do not name vaccines or medications. Your pediatrician will tell you the current schedule.*

---

## How to use the cards

- Print on cardstock if you can. Plain paper is fine.
- Each card is one well-baby visit. The card for the next visit goes in the diaper bag.
- Fill in the "what to prepare" and "questions I want to ask" sections the night before.
- Fill in the "what they said" and "what to do next" sections right after the visit, before you drive home.
- Keep the filled-in cards in a folder. After two years, you have a record.

A small detail: pediatric appointments are often shorter than the parent's questions. The card helps you ask the most important ones first.

---

## What to expect at every visit, in general

This is the rhythm that mostly applies to every well-baby visit. Specifics vary by office.

**The intake.** A nurse or medical assistant measures: weight, length (later height), head circumference (in the first two years). They may ask about feeding, sleep, and any concerns.

**The exam.** The pediatrician listens to heart and lungs. Looks in eyes, ears, mouth, throat. Feels the abdomen. Checks the hips. Checks the fontanelles (the soft spots) in babies. Checks reflexes in babies. Checks development through observation and questions.

**The development conversation.** They ask what your kid is doing. Movement, language, social. They may go through a checklist with you. Bring honesty, not what you wish were true.

**The recommendations.** Feeding guidance. Sleep guidance. Safety guidance. Vaccines, if it is a visit when vaccines are scheduled.

**The next visit.** They tell you when to come back. Sometimes you schedule on the way out.

**A note about vaccines.** Many well-baby visits include vaccines. Your pediatrician follows a recommended schedule. The schedule changes over time as research updates it. This planner does not name specific vaccines, because the schedule is what your pediatrician knows. Ask. Write down what was given today. The cards have a line for it.

## Card 1, the newborn / first-week visit

**Approximate age:** 3 to 5 days after discharge from the hospital.

**Why this visit happens so early:** Newborns lose weight in the first few days after birth and then start gaining it back. The pediatrician wants to see that the gain has started. Jaundice (yellowing) is also checked at this visit.

**WHAT THEY USUALLY CHECK:**

```
☐ Weight, length, head circumference
☐ Jaundice (skin and eye color, sometimes a blood test)
☐ The cord stump
☐ Heart and lungs
☐ Reflexes
☐ Feeding (breast / bottle / both): how often, how long, how it is going
☐ Wet and dirty diaper count over the last 24 hours
☐ The parent's mood and energy
```

**WHAT TO PREPARE:**

```
☐ How many wet diapers in the last 24 hours: ______
☐ How many dirty diapers in the last 24 hours: ______
☐ How often baby is feeding: ______
☐ Any color changes you noticed (skin, eyes, lips): ____________________
☐ Your top concern, in one sentence: ____________________
```

**WHAT TO ASK:**

```
- What is their weight relative to their birth weight?
- Is the jaundice normal for them, or do we need follow-up?
- Is the feeding pattern okay?
- When do I call you between now and the next visit?
```

**WHAT THEY SAID:**

```
Notes from the exam:
_______________________________________________
_______________________________________________
_______________________________________________

_______________________________________________

_______________________________________________

```

**RED FLAGS BEFORE THE NEXT VISIT (call the pediatrician):**

```
- Any fever (any temperature elevation in a newborn warrants a call)
- Very few or no wet diapers
- Significant weight loss continuing
- Yellowing of the skin or eyes that is getting worse
- Trouble breathing, fast or noisy breathing
- Hard to wake, or unusually limp
- Inconsolable crying for an extended stretch
- A sense that something is wrong
```

## When to call between visits

The cards cover scheduled visits. This page is for the calls between.

```
CALL THE PEDIATRICIAN'S OFFICE IF:

For a baby under 3 months:
☐ Any fever (any elevated temperature warrants a call)
☐ Trouble breathing, fast or noisy breathing
☐ Refusing to feed
☐ Significantly fewer wet diapers than usual
☐ Lethargic, hard to wake
☐ A rash that is spreading, especially if it does not blanch under pressure
☐ Inconsolable crying for more than an extended stretch

For an older baby or toddler:
☐ A fever above the threshold your pediatrician set, lasting longer than they told you
☐ Vomiting that does not stop, especially with fewer wet diapers
☐ Persistent diarrhea, especially with fewer wet diapers
☐ Trouble breathing, fast or noisy breathing, wheezing
☐ A rash that is spreading or does not blanch under pressure
☐ Lethargic, very floppy, hard to wake
☐ A head injury where you are not sure
☐ Possible exposure to medication, cleaning product, or unknown substance
   (Poison Control is also a fast call: in the US, 1-800-222-1222)
☐ Any allergic reaction (hives, swelling, breathing changes)
☐ Loss of skills they had before
☐ A change in behavior that worries you

For yourself:
☐ Postpartum mood is heavy, lasting longer than the first few weeks
☐ Thoughts of harm (yours or theirs)
☐ A sense you cannot do this
   These warrant a call to your own provider or the perinatal
   mental-health line, not the pediatrician, but the pediatrician
   is also a resource.
```

## A final note

Twelve cards. Two years. By the end of it, you will have a record of every measurement, every recommendation, every conversation that mattered.

It is also a record of you. The questions you brought to each visit. The things you noticed at home and remembered to mention. The dates you wrote down because you knew you would not remember them otherwise.

Keep the filled cards in a folder. The pediatrician changes practices, you move, you switch insurance. The folder is yours.

You are doing the work. They are growing.

*Soothemade*
