# Stay-at-Home Parent Transition Planner

*Thirty pages for the transition that everyone congratulates you on but nobody helps you plan.*

---

## A note before you start

You are leaving paid work to stay home. Or you have already left. Or you are about to.

The transition gets congratulated. It gets framed as "lucky" by the people still in offices. It gets framed as a "real job" by the people who have already done it. Both are partially true and neither is helpful for the actual planning.

What needs to be planned for is the money. The retirement account. The identity. The day-shape. The friend network that quietly disappears when you no longer share a hallway with people. The eventual re-entry, when and if you want one.

This planner is for the planning. It is not a feminist treatise. It is not a defense of the choice. The choice has been made or is being made by you and your family for your reasons. The planner does the work that comes after the choice.

*Soothemade*

> *This is a planning tool, not financial or legal advice. The financial and legal aspects of this transition should be reviewed by a financial planner and, if relevant, an attorney. The mental-health pages list crisis lines. Stay-at-home parents have elevated rates of depression and isolation. Knowing this in advance helps you watch for it.*

---

## How to use this planner

- Some pages are for you alone. Some are for the partner conversation.
- The "money" section, four pages, is meant to be done with the partner, not solo. Schedule a time. Bring food.
- The day-shape templates are by kid age. Find yours and start there.
- The re-entry section is at the back. You can ignore it for years. It is there for whenever it becomes relevant.

## The "I am still a person" page

A page to come back to.

```
PERMISSION SLIPS

You are still a person.
You did not become "just the mom" or "just the dad" the day you left
your job.
Your work history did not get erased when you stopped showing up at
the office.
Your interests, your sense of humor, your taste in music, the things
that bored you in meetings, the way you say "actually" before you
disagree with someone, all of those are still here.

You also are a parent, primarily, for these years. That part is real.

The work is holding both at once.

The next 30 pages help you do that.

WRITE DOWN, RIGHT NOW, THREE THINGS ABOUT YOU THAT HAVE NOTHING TO DO
WITH BEING A PARENT

   1. _______________________________________________
   2. _______________________________________________
   3. _______________________________________________

KEEP THIS PAGE. COME BACK TO IT.
```

## The partner money conversation, part 2: retirement

This is the page most planners skip.

```
WHEN ONE OF YOU STOPS EARNING, ONE OF YOU STOPS GETTING TAX-ADVANTAGED
RETIREMENT CONTRIBUTIONS FROM EMPLOYMENT.

   IF THIS IS NOT ADDRESSED, THE STAYING-HOME PARENT'S RETIREMENT
   ACCOUNT QUIETLY STAGNATES FOR THE YEARS THEY ARE HOME.

OPTIONS TO CONSIDER (with a financial planner, not just reading this)

   ☐ Spousal IRA: in many places, the working spouse can contribute
     to an IRA in the staying-home spouse's name. Check the current
     rules with a financial planner.

   ☐ Increased contributions to the working spouse's retirement
     account that are mentally earmarked for the household.

   ☐ Joint brokerage or investment accounts that grow during this
     period.

   ☐ Other tax-advantaged vehicles depending on your country.

THE NUMBER THE STAYING-HOME PARENT IS COMFORTABLE WITH

   How much per month / year is being contributed to retirement on the
   staying-home parent's behalf?

   $____________________

   The financial planner I am talking to about this:

   Name:                          ____________________
   Phone:                          ____________________
   Meeting date:                   ____________________

A NOTE: WOMEN AND OTHER PRIMARY CAREGIVERS HISTORICALLY ARRIVE AT
```

## The "what is my work now" reframe

A page for the days when the question "what do you do" feels harder than it used to.

```
THE OLD ANSWER

   Before, I would say:

   _______________________________________________

THE PROBLEM WITH "I AM A STAY-AT-HOME MOM/DAD"

   It is true, but it does not describe the work. It is also a
   shorthand that often invites:

   - The "oh, lucky you" reaction
   - The "must be nice" reaction
   - The condescending "what a wonderful gift to your kids" reaction
   - The "I could never do that" reaction

   None of those are conversations. They are conversation enders.

A FEW OTHER ANSWERS TO TRY

   - "I am running a small operation from my house."
   - "I am at home with the kids for a few years."
   - "I do the family logistics."
   - "I am between roles right now and managing the home."

PRACTICE WHAT I WANT TO SAY NEXT TIME

   _______________________________________________

WHAT I AM ACTUALLY DOING, IN PLAIN WORDS

   On a Tuesday, I:
   _______________________________________________
   _______________________________________________
   _______________________________________________

   That is real work. Write it down so you remember it on the days
   you forget.

THE LIST OF SKILLS I AM USING DAILY

   - Logistics (calendar, supplies, planning)
   - Conflict mediation (kid arguments, partner-and-me)
   - Project management (meal prep, household systems)
   - Crisis response (illnesses, behavior, weather)
   - Negotiation (with kids, with partner, with self)
   - Budgeting
   - Emotional labor coordination
   - Childcare expertise specific to your kids

THESE ARE REAL SKILLS. THEY EXIST ON RESUMES UNDER OTHER NAMES.
THEY DO NOT DISAPPEAR.
```

```
   ☐ Playgroups

   The first person I will message this week to schedule a hangout:
```

## The "I am not okay" page

Stay-at-home parents have elevated rates of depression, isolation, and at-home alcohol use. These are well-documented patterns. The planner names them honestly.

```
THE HONEST CHECK

   How many days this past week did I:

   - Get out of the house with the kid:                       ____
   - Talk to an adult who is not my partner:                  ____
   - Eat a meal sitting down at a table:                       ____
   - Cry, beyond a few tears:                                  ____
   - Drink more than I usually would:                          ____
   - Feel resentful of my partner specifically:                ____
   - Feel like "I am not built for this":                      ____
   - Feel like myself, recognizably:                           ____

   The pattern is concerning if:

   - Get out of the house = 0 or 1
   - Talk to an adult = 0 or 1
   - Crying = 5+
   - Drinking = 4+
   - Resentful = 4+
   - "Not built for this" = 4+
   - Feel like myself = 0 or 1

WHAT I AM NOTICING IN MYSELF

   _______________________________________________
   _______________________________________________

WHO I COULD TELL

   _______________________________________________

THE PROVIDER I AM RESERVING THE NUMBER FOR

   My primary care:                ____________________
   A therapist:                    ____________________
   A friend who has been through it:    _________________

CRISIS / SUPPORT LINES

   US, 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline
   US, Postpartum Support International: 1-833-852-6262
        (Note: still relevant up to a few years postpartum)
   US, SAMHSA National Helpline (substance use, free, 24/7):
        1-800-662-4357

   UK, Samaritans: 116 123

   Canada, 9-8-8 Suicide Crisis Helpline

   Australia, Lifeline: 13 11 14

A NOTE: ASKING FOR HELP IS NOT FAILURE. STAYING HOME IS A REAL JOB,
AND REAL JOBS HAVE BURNOUT RATES. YOURS DOES TOO. THE PEOPLE WHO
HELP YOU ARE NOT JUDGING YOU. ANYONE WHO WOULD JUDGE YOU IS NOT
THE PERSON YOU CALL.
```

## A final letter

You did something brave. You made a choice that is not easy to make.

The choice does not become easier on the page that says "made it." It becomes a job. A job that has stretches of joy that no office can match and stretches of tedium and isolation that no office prepares you for.

The planner is one of the things keeping you honest, with yourself and with your partner. Use it. Update it. Bring it to a couples-therapy session if you are in one. Revisit the re-entry page every year, or every few years, even if you are not planning to go back yet.

What you are doing matters. It is also tiring, and it is also not the entirety of who you are. The fourth thing on the list, "the part of you that has nothing to do with being a parent," is the part that will carry you through the long stretch. Tend it.

When the kids are grown, the planner is still here. The pages about you are still about you.

*Soothemade*
