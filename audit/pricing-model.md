# Pricing Model

## Anchor strategy

Use three anchors so every mid-priced item reads as the sensible middle choice, not the splurge.

1. The single-product anchor: P01 at $24. It is already the most expensive single and it stays there. Keep it visible near the top of the shop and in the sidebar of related listings. Once a $24 printable is the first number a shopper sees, the $14 planners and $12 card decks read as easy, and the $9 floor items read as almost free. P01 does not need to be the bestseller. It needs to be seen. Its only job is to set the ceiling for what a Soothemade printable can cost.

2. The bundle anchors: each themed kit lists its add-it-up single total right next to the kit price, in plain words. Birth Prep Kit, five items that come to $65, yours together for $42. The strike is honest because the singles really do sell at those prices. The $65 number makes the $42 kit the obvious move, and it makes any one $14 single inside it look small by comparison.

3. The catalog anchor: the megabundle, the whole shop, all 55 printables. List it at $189 against a real add-it-up total of $752. That is the largest number in the store, so it resets the whole price ladder downward in the shopper's head. Most people will not buy it, and that is fine. It exists so that a $42 themed kit feels modest and a $14 single feels like nothing. Show the megabundle on the shop landing and at the bottom of every bundle page, framed quietly: if you want the whole apothecary, here is the door. No countdown, no flashing, no fake scarcity. The size of the saving does the work, in keeping with a brand that lets the work sell itself.

Placement order matters. Lead with the high number, then reveal the cheaper options under it. High anchor first, then the field reads as a set of bargains rather than a list of costs.

## Tiering plan

Good, better, best, read as one calm ladder. The shopper meets a small, honest version first and can step up without ever feeling upsold.

GOOD, the singles ($9 to $16). One product, one need, bought at 11 p.m. on a phone. Three sub-rungs inside it. Floor at $9 (P02, P27, P42) holds the commodity checklists and short script packs low on purpose. These are entry doors and gifts, not where the margin lives. The $11 to $12 rung carries card decks, troubleshooting guides, and multi-page packs. The $14 rung is the center of gravity, the workhorse planners, journals, and workbooks, where most buyers land and where the catalog makes its money. The $16 rung is the singles ceiling for the deepest week-by-week planners and the clinical-niche journals: twins, IVF, NICU, adoption, birth anxiety, rainbow baby. A clean, legible ladder where the price tells you how much is inside.

BETTER, the themed kits ($35 to $49). Five or six related singles grouped by life moment and cluster: Birth Prep, First Six Weeks, Postpartum Mind, Boundary Scripts, Toddler Big Feelings, Pregnancy Collection, Loss and TTC, Eldercare, Return to Work. Each kit is priced at roughly two-thirds of its add-it-up single total, so the saving lands around the cost of two free items. The 12 ebooks (B01 to B12) ride along here as the warm extra inside the matching kit, the read-on-the-couch companion to the printables you fill in. The pitch is plain: you were going to buy two or three of these anyway, so here is the set for less. This is the rung that lifts average order value without raising a single sticker.

BEST, the megabundle ($189). The whole apothecary, all 55 printables, against a $752 add-it-up total. A small share of buyers take it, and they are worth a lot each. Its larger job is structural: it is the big number that makes the kits look modest and the singles look like nothing. One ladder, three honest steps, no pressure on any rung.
