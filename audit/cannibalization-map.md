# Cannibalization Map

Clusters where products are similar enough to confuse buyers. The fix is titles/positioning that make each one's distinct job obvious.

## Postpartum mind journals (the five that all say feelings)  (P03, P09, P21, P23, P29)

**Risk:** These are five postpartum journals that all live in the same search and all promise to help with the inside of a new mom's head, so a tired buyer skims them as one decision and picks the cheapest or the prettiest cover. P09 and P21 are the worst pair. P09's subtitle is Mood Tracker for the First Year and P21 calls itself a noticing log, so both read as the general postpartum anxiety journal even though P09 is a year-long mood log and P21 is for acute racing nights. P03 also gets swept in because it sits under postpartum mental health, but it is a slow-living planner and not a mood tool at all, so a buyer who wants to track their mood could buy P03 by mistake and feel let down.

**Fix:** Give each one a different trigger state and time horizon in the title so a buyer can self-sort by what is happening to them tonight. P09 is the wide, slow one: the whole first year, daily mood tracking, the honest is-this-more-than-the-blues check. Keep it as the Mind Journal and let it own breadth. P21 is the acute one: the 2am racing nights, grounding cards you use in the moment, and the signs that mean call today. Lead its title with the racing nights, not noticing, so it stops sounding like a general log. P23 owns one feeling the others tiptoe around: anger, mom rage, and the repair after the yelling. P29 is not about mood at all, it is about identity, who-am-I-now, matrescence, so position it as the self-you-lost journal, not another feelings tracker. P03 should drop out of the mental-health framing in its subtitle entirely and stand as a weekly slow-living planner for the tired weeks, so nobody buys it expecting a mood log. The line to hold across all five: P09 tracks the year, P21 holds the bad night, P23 is for the anger, P29 is for the lost self, P03 is the gentle weekly plan.

## What-to-say packs (visitors, the holiday table, and asking for help)  (P02, P25, P49, P15, B01, B02)

**Risk:** P02, P25, and P49 are all scripted-words products under boundary scripts, so they blur into one what-to-say pack and a buyer assumes buying one covers all the hard conversations. The valence is actually opposite across them and that is invisible from the titles. P02 and P25 are about keeping people at arm's length, while P49 is about pulling people in to help you, so a buyer who needs help asking for a meal might buy the visitor-boundary pack and get the wrong tool. P15 makes it worse: it is shelved next to the scripts and uses the same boundary language, but it is a printable door sign and meal-train page, not a script pack, so it reads as a fourth scripts product. The two ebooks B01 and B02 cover the same two topics again in long form, doubling the apparent overlap.

**Fix:** Sort them by who you are talking to and what you want from that person, and say that plainly in each title. P02 is for the front door in the newborn weeks: managing visitors, in-laws, and advice-givers when you need them to back off. P25 is one specific table you cannot get up from: the extended-family holiday meal, all year, not just the newborn season, so it does not compete with the postpartum-only P02. P49 is the opposite move and should say so: this is for asking people to show up with meals, childcare, and money, plus an easy way to say no, so it reads as the asking pack, not the deflecting pack. P15 should stop dressing as a script product and own being the thing you physically hang: a door sign plus a how-to-help and meal-train page, the welcome packet, so a buyer sees it as the print-and-tape companion to the scripts rather than a rival to them. Frame the set as a small shelf where each handles a different person: P02 for the visitor, P25 for the relative at the table, P49 for the friend who keeps saying let me know if you need anything, P15 for the door itself. The books are the read-it version of the same comfort and should cross-sell the matching printable, not stand in for it.

## Newborn feed and sleep logs for the foggy weeks  (P07, P11, P34, P36)

**Risk:** P07 and P11 are close to the same product on the shelf: both are printable newborn feed-and-sleep logs for the first weeks, both priced within a couple dollars, and a half-asleep buyer cannot tell why there are two. P34 and P36 get pulled into the same newborn-tracking search even though they do different jobs, so a parent who wants a simple daily log might land on the cry-decoder cards or the regression journal and feel like they bought the wrong thing.

**Fix:** Make P07 the full one and P11 the bare one, and say which is which. P07 is the complete daily feed-and-diaper log that also tells you what is normal, so it owns the reassurance angle: track it and check it against the reference. P11 is the stripped, cheaper, just-write-it-down log for the weeks too foggy to do more, so lead with foggy weeks and simple, and let price plus the absence of a reference section make it the lighter pick. P34 is not a log at all, it is interpretation: cue cards that help you read what the cry means, so position it as the decoder you reach for, not a tracker you fill in. P36 is a time-boxed pattern-finder, two weeks, explicitly not a method, so keep not a method in the subtitle and frame it as the thing you use to see the night-waking pattern before you talk to anyone, which keeps it clear of the sleep-method products it is not.

## Toddler big-feelings tools (the child's cards vs the parent's lines)  (P13, P16, P30, P23)

**Risk:** P13, P16, and P30 all sit in toddler emotions and all promise help with a kid who is melting down, so a parent buys one and assumes it covers tantrums, sibling fights, and naming feelings all at once. The real split is who uses the thing and in what fight, and the titles do not make that obvious. P23 shares the boundary-scripts and toddler tags but is actually about the parent's own anger, so it can get mistaken for another kid-behavior product.

**Fix:** Split them by who holds the tool and which conflict it is for. P13 is for the child: flash cards the kid looks at to name and calm a big feeling, so lead with the child naming it. P16 is for the parent's mouth in the heat of it: in-the-moment scripts to say during a tantrum on the kitchen floor, so lead with what to say. P30 is one specific fight the other two do not touch: kid versus kid, sibling fighting, the calm things to say when they will not stop, so keep sibling front and center. P23 is the odd one out and should be honest that it is about you, not the toddler: postpartum anger and the repair after you yell, which is why it also belongs with the mind journals above. The clean division: P13 the child holds, P16 the parent says, P30 is sibling-versus-sibling, P23 is the parent's own rage.

## Pregnancy keepsake and feelings journals  (P08, P53, P56)

**Risk:** All three are pregnancy journals in the keepsake and pregnancy-collection groups, and a gift buyer especially cannot tell them apart, so they grab whichever cover looks nicest and may end up with prompts when they wanted a record, or a record when they wanted a sentimental keepsake.

**Fix:** Name the form, not just the topic, because the form is the whole difference. P08 is the structured record: week-by-week, belly and milestones and appointments, the keepsake memory book you fill as it happens. P53 is feelings only: trimester-by-trimester emotional prompts with no logging or milestone tracking, so position it as the journal for what pregnancy feels like, not what week it is. P56 is the sentimental object: letters written to the baby before they arrive, which is a gift and a keepsake more than a tracker, so lead with the letters. The split a buyer should grasp at a glance: P08 records the weeks, P53 sits with the feelings, P56 writes to the baby.

## Birth-day documents (the plan, the VBAC plan, the fear, the bag)  (P14, P39, P52, P27)

**Risk:** P14 and P39 both produce a birth plan, so a buyer planning a VBAC could buy the general template (P14) and miss the one built for their exact situation (P39), or buy both and feel double-charged. P52 and P27 ride the same birth-prep search but do completely different jobs, the emotional one and the packing one, and a buyer who is scared of labor might buy a checklist that does not touch the fear.

**Fix:** Let each own one job on the way to the birth. P14 is the universal fillable plan you hand the nurse: one-page summary, c-section section included, for any birth, so it is the default document. P39 is for one specific path the default does not cover well: VBAC and repeat-cesarean, the decision and the questions for your doctor, so lead with VBAC so the right buyer self-selects and the wrong buyer is not lured into it. P52 is not a logistics doc at all, it is the workbook for the fear of giving birth, so keep it clearly emotional and away from the plan-template language. P27 is the suitcase: what to pack for mom, partner, and baby, so it stays the checklist and nothing more. Said simply: P14 is the plan everyone can use, P39 is the plan for a VBAC, P52 is for the fear, P27 is for the bag.

## Maternity-leave and working-parent planners  (P54, P31, P17, P47)

**Risk:** P54 and P31 both read as maternity-leave-and-work planners and a buyer cannot tell that one is for going out and the other is for coming back, so someone about to tell their boss might buy the return-to-work planner by mistake, or buy both without realizing they are two ends of the same arc. P17 is also a work product but only about pumping, and P47 is the opposite decision entirely, not returning, so it can get lost in the same return-to-work search.

**Fix:** Anchor each to a point on the timeline and make that the title's job. P54 is the front end, before you leave: telling your boss, HR scripts, the handoff template, pregnant-at-work, so lead with telling your boss. P31 is the back end, after you return: the first 90 days back, the re-entry plan, so lead with back to work and first 90 days so it cannot be confused with the leaving one. P17 is narrow and should stay narrow: the pumping schedule for the day before and every day after you return, the logistics piece that pairs with P31 rather than competing with it. P47 is the road not taken and should say so plainly: staying home, one income, money and identity for the parent who is not going back, so it reads as the alternative to this whole group, not a member of it. The arc to make legible: P54 is leaving, P31 is returning, P17 is the pump, P47 is staying home.

## Loss, fertility, and the pregnancy after  (P20, P40, P35, P41, P48)

**Risk:** These share the loss-and-grief and fertility-ttc tags and a buyer in a tender place may not parse which stage each one meets, which is the costliest possible mix-up in the catalog. P20 and P40 are the sharpest risk: one is for grieving a loss, the other is for being pregnant again after one, and a grieving buyer handed the rainbow-baby planner by accident would be hurt, not helped. P35 and P41 are both for trying to conceive but one is a full medical IVF cycle and one is a quiet plain notebook, so a buyer not doing IVF might land on the clinical one. P48 is the adoption path and can get swept into the same fertility search even though it is a different road.

**Fix:** Be unmistakable about the stage in every title, gently, because here a wrong purchase lands on someone already grieving. P20 is after the loss: the grief and the keepsake, any week, so it stays clearly the after-the-loss companion. P40 is the next chapter and must read as such: pregnant again after a loss, the rainbow baby, a calm place for the second story, so no grieving buyer mistakes it for the grief journal and no expecting buyer mistakes the grief journal for it. P35 is one active IVF cycle: stim tracker, two-week wait, beta day, so it names the medical path and only that path. P41 is the quiet everyday one: discreet TTC and cycle notes in a plain notebook that does not look like fertility tracking, so lead with discreet and plain so the non-IVF buyer recognizes it as theirs. P48 is its own road, adoption and the home study, the pregnancy that does not show, so it is positioned as the path beside the others, not a fertility tracker. The stages, kept distinct: P20 after the loss, P40 the pregnancy after, P35 the IVF cycle, P41 quiet trying, P48 adoption.

## Eldercare planners (the two near-identical titles)  (P46, P50, B11, B12)

**Risk:** This is the tightest collision in the catalog. P46 and P50 are both Caregiver Planner for Aging Parents, both about eldercare, both the same price, and the only thing telling them apart is a subtitle most buyers will not read, so a person caring for a parent sees two nearly identical listings and either buys the wrong phase or buys neither out of confusion. The two books B11 and B12 mirror them exactly, the first 90 days and the long stretch, so the same confusion repeats one shelf over.

**Fix:** Make the time phase the entire identity of each, starting with the first words of the title, so the two stop looking like duplicates of one product. P46 is the acute onset: the hospital discharge, assembling the medical team, the legal paperwork, the first 90 days when everything is new and on fire. Lead with the crisis and the first 90 days. P50 is the chronic grind that comes after: the long haul, the multi-year slow decline, sustainability and burnout when there is no discharge date. Lead with the long stretch and the long haul. A buyer should be able to tell from the cover alone whether they are in week one or year three. Carry the exact same split through the books so B11 stays the first-90-days read and B12 stays the long-haul read, and let each book point to its matching planner so the pair reads as read-then-do, not as two versions of the same thing.

## Twin pregnancy vs twin postpartum  (P33, P45)

**Risk:** Lower risk than the others, but real: both lead with Twin and Planner, so a skimming twin parent might grab one assuming it carries them from pregnancy through the newborn weeks, when one stops at birth and the other starts there.

**Fix:** Put the before-and-after-birth line where it cannot be missed. P33 is everything up to the birth: twin pregnancy, week by week, the pregnancy the single-baby planners do not cover. P45 starts the day they come home: the first six weeks home with two, feeding and diaper logs, recovery, the night schedule. Make pregnancy and the first six weeks home the loudest words in each title so a buyer knows which side of the birth they are on, and so the two read as a matched set a twin parent might want both of, in order, rather than a redundant pick-one.

## Books that shadow their parent printable  (B07, P03, B08, P04, B06, P52, B10, P07, B05, P51, B04, P13)

**Risk:** Each book was built from a printable and shares its name and topic, so the catalog now has matched pairs that look like the same product twice. Slow Motherhood the book (B07) next to the Slow Motherhood Planner (P03), the Partner's Playbook as both a book (B08) and a printable (P04), Birth Is Scary (B06) beside the Fear of Childbirth Workbook (P52), and so on. A buyer who wants one comfort sees two listings with nearly the same name and cannot tell whether they are buying a duplicate, so they buy one and assume they have the whole thing, or stall on the choice.

**Fix:** Position format as the job, the same way across every pair, so the book and the printable complement instead of compete. The book is the read-once one: the why and the story, the thing you read on your phone at 2am while feeding, no printing, no filling in. The printable is the do-it one: the worktool you print and write in, the pages and logs and checklists. Make the pairing explicit in the listings so they cross-sell each other on purpose. Read Slow Motherhood, then use the Slow Motherhood Planner. Read Birth Is Scary, then fill in the Fear of Childbirth Workbook. The book opens the door and the printable is what you do once you are through it, which turns every near-duplicate name into a deliberate read-then-do set and lets a buyer happily own both.
